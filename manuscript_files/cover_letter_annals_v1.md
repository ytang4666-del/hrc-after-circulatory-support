Dear Editors of Annals of Intensive Care,

We are pleased to submit our Research Article entitled "Hemodynamic Recovery Capacity After Early Circulatory Support Across Three Critical Care Databases" for consideration in Annals of Intensive Care.

Early circulatory support is often judged by restoration of mean arterial pressure, vasopressor trends, urine output, lactate, and clinician assessment. However, restoration of macrocirculatory pressure does not necessarily indicate organ-level recovery. In this study, we developed Hemodynamic Recovery Capacity (HRC), a model-based residualized measure of post-support physiologic recovery, and evaluated whether it identifies a reproducible recovery phenotype after early circulatory support.

The study is based on three independent critical care databases: MIMIC-IV v3.1, eICU-CRD v2.0, and SICdb v1.0.8. Across 16,757 patients in MIMIC-IV, 9,275 in eICU, and 8,274 in SICdb, higher HRC was consistently associated with lower post-landmark hospital mortality and lower odds of organ nonrecovery. The phenotype distinguished pressure restoration from broader organ recovery and was supported by MIMIC-IV mechanistic analyses involving central venous pressure, mean perfusion pressure, cardiogenic shock, and mechanical circulatory support.

We believe the manuscript is well aligned with Annals of Intensive Care because it addresses hemodynamic monitoring, circulatory support, organ perfusion, and outcome assessment in critically ill patients. It also builds directly on database-based hemodynamic studies previously published in Annals of Intensive Care, including studies of MAP, blood pressure components, and central venous pressure, while extending this literature from isolated hemodynamic exposures to a cross-database dynamic recovery phenotype.
